Rebar Measurement & Evaluation Plan

Draft v1 · July 2026 · Peer-reviewed instruments embedded in the Level Check · Companion: Aggregate Report format sample

Rebar's promise is practical: take weight off construction workers and the families behind them. To prove that — to funders, partners, and ourselves — the data we gather has to be built on instruments the research community already trusts. This plan lays out the validated short-form instruments now embedded in Rebar's quarterly Level Check, why each was chosen, how each is scored, and what the readings mean.

1 · Design principles

  • Verbatim items. Each instrument's questions appear word for word (with warm framing around them, never inside them). Changing item wording voids the validation, so we don't.
  • Ultra-brief forms. Total burden is 17 items (~10 minutes), using the shortest validated version of each construct. Long batteries kill completion in this population.
  • Screen, don't diagnose. Every instrument here is a screener. A flag routes the user to a concrete punchlist action and, where warranted, to 988/SAMHSA — it never labels them.
  • Actionable by design. Every domain measured maps to a Rebar scope, so an amber reading always has a next move inside the tool.
  • Spanish parity. Validated Spanish translations are used for every instrument, matching Rebar's bilingual UI.

3 · The instruments, academically

WHO-5 Well-Being Index

Five positively-phrased items on the past two weeks, each rated 0–5; the raw sum (0–25) is multiplied by 4 to give a 0–100 score. A score ≤ 50 indicates reduced wellbeing warranting follow-up; ≤ 28 suggests likely depression. The WHO-5 is among the most widely used brief wellbeing measures in the world, with a systematic review of 213 studies supporting its validity as an outcome measure, sensitivity to change over time, and utility as a depression screen. It is free to use and exists in validated translations in over 30 languages, including Spanish.

Why for Rebar: it is our primary outcome measure. Positively worded (no deficit framing — important for a stigma-sensitive audience), sensitive to change, and comparable across programs, so retreat cohorts and Rebar users can be tracked on the same scale.

Topp CW, Østergaard SD, Søndergaard S, Bech P. The WHO-5 Well-Being Index: a systematic review of the literature. Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics. 2015;84(3):167–176.

PHQ-2 (Patient Health Questionnaire-2)

The first two items of the PHQ-9 — anhedonia and depressed mood over two weeks — scored 0–3 each. At the standard cutoff of ≥ 3, the PHQ-2 showed 83% sensitivity and 92% specificity for major depressive disorder in the original validation (n = 6,000 primary-care and OB-GYN patients). It is a public-domain screener recommended widely for first-stage screening.

Why for Rebar: construction has among the highest suicide rates of any industry; a two-item screen is the least invasive credible way to watch this. We deliberately use the PHQ-2 rather than PHQ-9 to avoid administering a self-harm item in an unmoderated tool; a positive screen surfaces 988 and warm referral language instead.

Kroenke K, Spitzer RL, Williams JB. The Patient Health Questionnaire-2: validity of a two-item depression screener. Medical Care. 2003;41(11):1284–1292.

GAD-2 (Generalized Anxiety Disorder-2)

The two core items of the GAD-7, scored 0–3 each; a cutoff of ≥ 3 yields 86% sensitivity and 83% specificity for generalized anxiety disorder, and performs acceptably as a broader screen for panic, social anxiety, and PTSD.

Why for Rebar: anxiety (money, job security, family strain) is the load our users name most often; pairing GAD-2 with PHQ-2 gives the standard, publishable "PHQ-4" style mental-health snapshot at four items total.

Kroenke K, Spitzer RL, Williams JB, Monahan PO, Löwe B. Anxiety disorders in primary care: prevalence, impairment, comorbidity, and detection. Annals of Internal Medicine. 2007;146(5):317–325.

UCLA Loneliness Scale — 3-item short form

Three items (lack companionship, feel left out, feel isolated) rated 1–3; total 3–9, with ≥ 6 commonly used to classify "lonely." Developed for large telephone surveys from the Revised UCLA Loneliness Scale, with satisfactory internal consistency (α ≈ .72) and strong correlation with the full scale. Used in major aging and health studies (e.g., HRS/NSHAP).

Why for Rebar: disconnection is Project BUILT's core theory of the crisis — "people don't leave jobs, they leave people." This is the standard brief measure of exactly that construct, and our most important mediating variable: if Rebar works the way we believe, UCLA-3 should move first and mental-health scores should follow.

Hughes ME, Waite LJ, Hawkley LC, Cacioppo JT. A short scale for measuring loneliness in large surveys: results from two population-based studies. Research on Aging. 2004;26(6):655–672.

Sleep duration — BRFSS single item

"On average, how many hours of sleep do you get in a 24-hour period?" — the CDC Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System item, enabling direct comparison to national and occupational norms. The joint AASM/Sleep Research Society consensus recommends ≥ 7 hours per night for adults; < 7 hours is associated with impaired safety-critical performance, injury risk, and adverse mental and physical health outcomes.

Why for Rebar: sleep is the co-founders' "do you get 7 hours?" question, made comparable to CDC surveillance data. A reading under 7 auto-offers the "Seven hours, protected" punchlist item — the tightest question-to-action loop in the tool. It also mirrors the yes/no body check inside the Body scope, giving a behavioral cross-check.

Watson NF, et al. Recommended amount of sleep for a healthy adult: a joint consensus statement of the American Academy of Sleep Medicine and Sleep Research Society. Sleep. 2015;38(6):843–844. · CDC BRFSS Questionnaire, sleep module.

Financial fragility — FINRA NFCS "$2,000" item

"How confident are you that you could come up with $2,000 if an unexpected need arose within the next month?" — the canonical single-item measure of household financial fragility from the FINRA National Financial Capability Study, introduced by Lusardi, Schneider & Tufano. Respondents answering "probably not" or "certainly not" are classified financially fragile; the item predicts hardship better than income alone because it captures access to all coping resources.

Why for Rebar: "the cliff" (income stopping cold between jobs) is the Money scope's central problem. This item measures resilience to exactly that shock, in one question, with a national benchmark to compare our population against.

Lusardi A, Schneider D, Tufano P. Financially fragile households: evidence and implications. Brookings Papers on Economic Activity. Spring 2011:83–134.

AUDIT-C (Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test — Consumption)

The three consumption items of the WHO's AUDIT, scored 0–4 each (0–12 total). In the original VA validation, ≥ 4 for men (≥ 3 for women) identified heavy drinking and/or active abuse or dependence with performance comparable to the full AUDIT. Because Rebar does not collect gender, we flag conservatively at ≥ 3 with soft, non-judgmental language.

Why for Rebar: substance use is one of Project BUILT's four named crisis threads. AUDIT-C is the standard brief consumption screen, and making the section explicitly skippable preserves trust — a skipped section is recorded as missing data, not assumed zero.

Bush K, Kivlahan DR, McDonell MB, Fihn SD, Bradley KA. The AUDIT alcohol consumption questions (AUDIT-C): an effective brief screening test for problem drinking. Archives of Internal Medicine. 1998;158(16):1789–1795.

4 · Cadence & how it lives in the product

  • Baseline: the Level Check is offered from the home screen; the first completion is the user's baseline record.
  • Every 90 days: the home card flips to "due" and invites a re-run. Each run is appended to the user's record (profile.levels[]), enabling within-person change scores — the design funders actually want (pre/post, not just cross-sectional).
  • Daily pulse (Good / Getting by / Rough): retained as a lightweight engagement and momentary-affect signal; it is not a validated instrument and is analyzed only as usage/context data.
  • Body quick check (4 yes/no items): behavioral self-report tied directly to punchlist actions; the sleep item cross-checks the BRFSS reading.
  • Work items (2, optional, workers only): missed workdays in the past month and near-miss on the job — self-reported, in the product's own voice, never shown to employers. These join wellbeing data to project-delivery outcomes, the argument owners and boards respond to. Optional trade / years-in / project-type chips at setup enable cohort slicing (e.g., data-center vs manufacturing crews).
  • Flag → action: every amber gauge offers one concrete punchlist item, and mood flags surface 988. The measurement layer and the intervention layer are the same surface.

5 · What we can claim with this data

With baseline + quarterly follow-ups we can report, in academically defensible terms: mean WHO-5 change over time (primary outcome); movement across published screening thresholds (PHQ-2, GAD-2, UCLA-3, AUDIT-C); % of users below the 7-hour sleep consensus and their change; and financial-fragility prevalence vs. FINRA national benchmarks. Loneliness (UCLA-3) serves as the hypothesized mediator consistent with Project BUILT's theory of change. Retreat families and Rebar-only users share the same battery, so the retreat's two-year follow-along can be evaluated on the same scales.

Limitations to state up front: these are self-report screeners, not diagnoses; the sample is self-selected; without a comparison group, change scores show association, not causation. For grant-grade evaluation, a waitlist or staggered-onboarding comparison is the natural next step — worth designing with Réka before the user base grows.

C · Daily pulse (shown once per day at sign-in)

"How are you doing today?" with three options: Good · Getting by · Rough. "Rough" surfaces 988 and peer support immediately. Analyzed as engagement and momentary-affect context only; it is not a validated instrument and feeds no scores.

7 · Data governance

  • In the current prototype, responses persist only in the user's own account (local device storage); nothing is transmitted.
  • Production plan: item-level responses stored against the account; Project BUILT staff and research partners see only de-identified, aggregated exports. No individual scores are ever shown to employers, foremen, or crews — this is stated in-product.
  • Consent language for research use of aggregate data should be added at account creation before any aggregate reporting; IRB or ethics review advisable before publication.
  • All instruments used are free for use (public domain or freely licensed); no licensing fees apply.
